## Think before you build

1. **Who lands on this page?** A municipal decision-maker — a Bürgermeister of a small to mid-size city, a Stadtmarketing-Leiter, a Wirtschaftsförderung director, a Stadtwerke executive, a Liegenschaftsamt referent, or a city council member exploring digital infrastructure. They are nearly unaware: most don't know that an advertising-funded screen model exists where the city earns money instead of paying for screens. They are cautious by role: public budgets, public scrutiny, multi-year decision cycles.

2. **What is the one thought they need to have to inquire?** "This isn't a cost — it's a revenue source. There's no investment risk. Other cities are already doing this. We could pilot one location to test it. The advertising won't embarrass the city."

3. **What would actually make them inquire?** Real proof of cities that did this. A clear revenue model with realistic numbers (e.g. 20% to the city until amortized, then 35%). Reassurance about advertising standards (no inappropriate content). Visible permit/legal handling. The ability to start with one pilot location.

4. **What would stop them?** Anything that smells like vendor risk. Any sense that the city has to invest. Worry about advertising taste / public reaction. Unclear permit responsibilities. No comparable references. A pitch that feels too commercial for a public-sector audience.

Now design the page that answers (3) and removes (4).

## Elevation permission

Sections worth considering:

- A **pilot location offer** — explicit framing: start with one location, evaluate over X months, then decide on rollout. Removes commitment fear for risk-averse public-sector decision-makers.
- A **public benefit framing** — section emphasizing the civic angle: emergency communication capability, local advertiser support, reduced print waste, modernized city image
- A **municipal references map / list** — even if cities can't be named, indicating "we work with cities of X size in Y region" provides validation
- A **city information vs advertising ratio** — visual showing typical content split (e.g. 30% city content / 70% advertising)
- A **typical revenue range chart** — what cities of different sizes typically earn (small: X €/month, mid: Y, large: Z) marked as orientation
- A **permit timeline** — typical permit process from agreement to running screen, so the decision-maker can plan internally

Add only if it elevates. Drop or replace if you have a better section — explain in HTML comment.

## Depth instructions

### Use cases section
Reason through these 4 municipal scenarios with real public-sector specificity:

- **Kleine Gemeinde (5–20k Einwohner):** Limited foot traffic but real local advertiser interest (lokales Handwerk, regionaler Handel). One indoor stele in the Rathaus foyer, simpler revenue model. What does the agreement look like? What's the realistic revenue?
- **Mittelstadt (20–100k Einwohner):** Fußgängerzone with multiple Outdoor-Stelen, city events promoted alongside local advertising. Stadtmarketing-organisation typically the partner. What's the rollout strategy?
- **Großstadt-Bezirk:** Larger network potential, mixed indoor/outdoor, partnership with city administration + BID/Stadtmarketing. How is revenue distributed across multiple stakeholders?
- **Stadtwerke / kommunaler Eigenbetrieb:** Different organizational structure, screens at Bäder/Bibliotheken/Bürgerservice, internal communication + commercial advertising mix. Different decision pathway.

Each card: municipality type label + scenario headline + 3–4 sentences with public-sector specificity (decision pathway, stakeholder structure, realistic revenue context).

### Testimonials section
Three voices from the municipal world. The right register sounds careful and substantive:

- A Bürgermeister of a smaller city talking about how the conversation with the council went (e.g. "Anfangs war der Stadtrat skeptisch — kein Risiko und Werbeeinnahmen klang zu gut. Nach dem ersten Quartal mit dem Pilot in der Fußgängerzone war die Verlängerung einstimmig.")
- A Stadtmarketing-Leiterin talking about the practical operational side (e.g. "Was uns überzeugt hat: HYSN hat die Genehmigung beim Bauamt selbst durchgebracht — wir mussten nur den Standort freigeben.")
- A Wirtschaftsförderungs-Direktor talking about the local business angle (e.g. "Die lokalen Händler bekommen Werbeflächen, die sie sich bei klassischen Werbetafeln nicht leisten konnten. Das war für uns der entscheidende Punkt.")

German, public-sector register — careful, substantive, no marketing language.

### Vorher/Nachher case study
Construct a representative municipal transformation. Vorher: print noticeboards, no advertising revenue, modernization gap. Nachher: digital steles operating, monthly revenue to the city, modernized city image. Real or representative numbers.

### FAQ
Real municipal decision-maker questions: who applies for permits, what content is allowed (and what's not — e.g. no political advertising during election periods, no alcohol near schools), what happens at end of contract, who pays for electricity, what's the council approval pathway, can we cancel if it doesn't work, can we control which advertisers run, who is liable for damage, what's the Datenschutz situation with cameras/sensors, can we use it for emergency communication.

## Wireframe style rules — non-negotiable

- Single self-contained HTML file. No external dependencies.
- Grayscale palette: `#ffffff`, `#f4f4f4`, `#d0d0d0`, `#333333`, `#111111`
- Body `#f0f0f0`, page wrapper `#ffffff`, max-width 900px centered, 250px right margin
- Font: `system-ui, -apple-system, sans-serif`
- Section labels: `[SECTION NAME]` 10px uppercase gray
- Image placeholders: gray rectangle with diagonal SVG cross + German caption
- Buttons: 2px solid #333, no fill, padding 12px 28px
- Annotations: sticky-note boxes right margin, every section gets one
- Sections separated by 1px #d0d0d0 border
- Desktop only
